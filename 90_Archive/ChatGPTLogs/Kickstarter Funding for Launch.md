Yes, it’s common for entrepreneurs launching products via crowdfunding platforms like Kickstarter to include a personal salary in their funding goals. This allows them to focus full-time on the product without needing another job. Here's how it typically works:

---

### **How Including a Salary Works**

1. **Transparent Budgeting:**
   - Entrepreneurs usually calculate a budget that includes their personal living expenses alongside costs like prototyping, manufacturing, marketing, and fulfillment.
   - On Kickstarter, you can communicate to backers that a portion of the funds will go toward compensating you for your time and effort to deliver the project.

2. **Salary Considerations:**
   - The salary is typically modest and designed to cover living expenses for the development and delivery phase (often 6–12 months).
   - The goal is to ensure you can dedicate yourself fully to the project without financial distractions.

3. **Breakdown Example:**
   - If you estimate $50,000 for product costs and $45,000 for living expenses, your funding goal would be $95,000, plus a buffer for unexpected costs.

4. **Full-Time Focus:**
   - By paying yourself, you eliminate the need for a secondary income source, allowing for faster development and better results for backers.

---

### **How to Justify This to Backers**

1. **Transparency:**
   - Clearly outline in your campaign how funds will be allocated. Many creators include a pie chart or detailed list.
   - Explain that the salary enables you to work full-time on the project and ensures its success.

2. **Value Proposition:**
   - Emphasize how your full-time focus improves the quality, timeline, and overall delivery of the product.
   - Backers are usually supportive as long as they feel their contributions directly lead to the promised product.

---

### **Risks and Considerations**

1. **Overpromising:**
   - Avoid underestimating costs or overpromising on deliverables. Campaigns that don’t deliver can damage your reputation.

2. **Taxes and Fees:**
   - A significant portion of funds (up to 10–20%) may go to platform fees (Kickstarter, Stripe, etc.) and taxes. Factor these into your funding goal.

3. **Cash Flow Management:**
   - Backers pledge upfront, but you often only receive funds after the campaign ends, so you'll need a clear timeline for when money will be available.

---

### **Post-Kickstarter Options**

1. **Stretch Goals:**
   - Use additional funding to extend your runway or add features to the product.

2. **Revenue After Launch:**
   - After fulfilling Kickstarter orders, many creators shift to direct sales, opening new revenue streams to sustain themselves.

3. **Partnerships:**
   - A successful Kickstarter can attract partnerships, investors, or licensing deals, providing further financial support.

---

Including a salary is an industry norm when launching a product, but it must be communicated clearly and responsibly. Transparency builds trust, and backers are usually happy to support creators who are fully dedicated to their projects.
