ELEVATOR PITCH / ONE LINER

A physical instrument for electronic improvisation; enabling gestural control of parametric musical structures to compose emotional arcs in real time.


DESCRIPTION / ABSTRACT

Mutax is the first step towards realizing my vision of a hardware performance instrument designed specifically for fluid improvisation of live electronic music. Existing electronic instruments often lack crucial features for live creation – they tend to be targeted towards playing back pre-composed patterns and typically lack the ability to make simultaneous changes across many tracks at once, making real-time composition cumbersome and limiting . More critically, electronic instruments often  lack a meaningful connection between physical gesture and sound, unlike traditional acoustic instruments where expression and gesture are inherently linked. Mutax attempts to address both of these problems: it enables simultaneous control over core musical elements – rhythm, harmony, texture, and structure – through an interface rooted in expressive, tactile gestures. This restores a sense of immediacy, spontaneity and embodiment to electronic music performance, allowing the user to quickly generate and record musical ideas and fluidly move them to new places with a single gesture. When playing Mutax, the user simultaneously embodies the roles of both a composer and a conductor, with their movements directly guiding the dynamics of their MIDI orchestra, for whom they are composing the score in real time.

BACKGROUND / RESEARCH (200 - 400 words)

A lot of my research for this project came in the form of personal experiences that I had prior to applying to this program. Years of acquiring, playing, touring with, and troubleshooting various hardware electronic instrument setups led me to the conclusion that I would need to develop my own solution to the various problems I was encountering. Through my experiences with these various gear set ups, I found that the main problems were due to the fact that controlling and managing multiple instruments at once with only two hands and the pressure of a live audience becomes overwhelming quickly. Especially when attempting to make sweeping changes to the music, like transitioning to a new uncharted song section which requires controlling many parameters at once. Creating music from scratch in real time is hard enough, but composing and shifting that music to new places by manually adjusting individual parameters one at a time can quickly become repetitive and boring for an audience, and overwhelming for the performer. 

In my research into other peoples’ solutions to similar problems, I have encountered a few key artists whose work has a major influence on my own. It is worth noting that almost every other artist I have found who is also attempting to improvise electronic music live has had to develop their own bespoke system to do so — there are seemingly no complete off-the-shelf solutions to this particular set of problems. Notable examples include UK beatboxer Beardyman and his highly complex Beardytron system - a custom live-looping setup consisting of 5 iPads with touch interfaces for the various elements of his system, Tim Exile’s Flow Machine, Endlesss and Scapeshift – the evolution of his own Reaktor-based live performance system into an iOS and Desktop app and most recently a generative, parameter morphing generative groovebox, and Onyx Ashanti and his 3D-printed, Raspberry Pi and Pure Data based wearable performance instrument which has grown in complexity and nuance over the course of the last 20 years.

These artists have pioneered the various methods that are combined within my system. Retroactive looping has existed in the systems of Tim Exile and Beardyman for decades. Both of these artists’ systems also have methods for creating new song sections and making sweeping changes on the fly: the Beardytron contains an array of pre-made effect parameter automations that allow him to instantly create new song sections (ie a classic dnb drop, a techno breakdown, etc) out of his existing looper material by simultaneously applying per-track effects to chop, filter, and remix the loops. Scapeshift is completely built around the concept of shifting generative music. Scapeshift allows the user to move the generated material by either adjusting macro parameters like density and space, tossing the dice towards one of the predefined “style” directions, which probabilistically weighs the sequencer and synthesizer parameters towards certain genres or vibes like ambient or IDM, or playing back an arrangement of saved patterns, which are simply stored global parametric states that are interpolated between to morph from one musical place to another. While my implementation of some of these concepts is quite different in practice, the precedent set by these artists heavily guided my development of this project.

IMPLEMENTATION / TECHNICAL DETAILS 

At the moment, Mutax is implemented as a physical prototype based on the Teensy 4.1 microcontroller. The teensy is hosted on the ProtoSupples Project System baseboard, a prototyping PCB that breaks out all of the Teensy’s pins to headers, adds MIDI IO, a USB host port, a large TFT touch display and many other conveniences. The prototype contains an array of Adafruit modules, including 3 5x6 neokey matrices, 3 TCA8418 key matrix scanner IC breakout boards, 9 quad rotary encoder boards, and a Nintendo Wiichuck I2c breakout board which are all soldered to two perfboards that are plugged into the header pins on the Project System baseboard. The current implementation of the firmware, written in C++ with the assistance of multiple AI coding tools such as Gemini 2.5 Pro, Claude 3.7, and Chat GPT o1 in the Cursor IDE, contains a retroactive MIDI looper, an algorithmic sequencer, and a macro-level “archetype” engine which allows for generative control over all sequencer and looper tracks simultaneously, facilitated by gestures via the Wiichuck controller. The firmware generates midi clock internally (or listens and syncs to external clock) via the uClock library, and listens to all incoming MIDI data, time stamping it and storing it in a circular buffer on external PSRAM. At any point after the user plays something they like, they can select a loop length button which grabs the selected length of content from the buffer (plus an extra amount of content for loop-start offsetting if needed) and immediately loops it, applying any added modifiers (transposition, note probability, velocity offset, quantization, etc.) before note playback. The algorithmic sequencer is controlled via the rotary encoders and is based on the Euclidean algorithm, with an additional distribution parameter which weights the Euclidean pulses towards the beginning or end of the steps. The archetype mode button opens a menu on the mechanical key matrix which allows the user to select from an array of archetypal musical changes to apply to the looper/sequencer content, split into the categories of harmonic and energetic. The harmonic options are arranged as scale degrees and chord types (based on the current global scale) and the energy shift options at the moment contain: increased energy, decreased energy, breakdown (remove drums), and 4 on the floor. Selecting options from these lists cues them in the system, and when the Z. Button on the Wiichuck is pressed and held, the system enters a transition state where the accelerometer controls sequencer pulses and the joystick controls melodic octave transposition, allowing the user to gesturally perform fills until they decide to release the Z button, at which point the cued up harmonic and energetic changes are applied to all tracks simultaneously. 

CONCLUSION / ADDITIONAL INFO

This project has really been about 7 years in the making, beginning back in 2018 when I first started my journey of trying to find/create a live performance system that would allow me to improvise. I initially started with some Elektron boxes, the Digitakt and Digitone, which I thoroughly enjoyed using. However, the workflow of these devices was heavily tailored towards pre-production, rather than live creation, since their sequencers are so deep and require time to craft an interesting pattern. After your pattern is created, there are tools that allow for performing these patterns such as manipulating the synth/sampler parameters, using the control all feature, and also performatively using the func+no button to snap back to a familiar place after thoroughly messing up the pattern. With all of these tools, it was still not feasible to improvise a live set from scratch on these devices — you would have to pre-program some banks of patterns to play through and manipulate, which is not a workflow that I was satisfied with. From these Elektron sequencers I moved on to a much more live-friendly sequencer, the Torso Electronics T-1 Algorithmic Sequencer. This piece of gear truly opened my eyes to the possibilities of live electronic improvisation, because for the first time I was able to start a performance from scratch and quickly build and change musical ideas by just adjusting parameters, rather than the common sequencer paradigm which felt more like programming than playing. With this device I felt like I was getting closer to my goal of being able to completely improvise my live sets, however there were still some issues with my setup overall: I was unable to easily move the music as a whole — making changes still involved individual manual parameter adjustments across many tracks, which meant that getting from point A to point B musically was a multi-step process, not something I could do in one fell swoop.

During the Fall semester of IMA Low Res, I took a class called Critical Experiences, during which we did a daily exercise of our own choosing to gain creative insights. I chose to do a daily improvisation with my existing set up with the Torso T-1, and most importantly I would take notes whenever there was something I wanted to do to the music that I was physically unable to do with the equipment in front of me. This exercise was very illuminating and set into motion the overall direction of Mutax when I realized that almost all of the changes I wanted to make that I wasn’t able to do had to do with shifts in the energy or emotional quality of the music. I wanted to be able to do build-ups and drops, increase or decrease the overall intensity, shift the harmonic direction, etc. and I was struggling to do so with the limitations of the equipment I was using. This is where Mutax gets its name from — “Mut” as a prefix refers to change, like mutability, and “ax” as a suffix means “a state of” — so Mutax is a system that is always in a state of change.

The development of this project began with my final project for Critical Experiences, which was a retroactive MIDI looper based on the Teensy 4.1. I knew that before I could implement any of these ideas around musical changes, I would need a stable platform to build the musical ideas that these changes would be applied to. Looping has long been a common method of building musical ideas live, and retroactive looping is an even more freeing version of it because it removes the performance anxiety of the red record button. Instead of having to press record and then nail the perfect take, a retroactive looper is always recording, so that the user can just freely play and improvise without worrying about getting it perfect, and AFTER they play something they liked, they can capture the loop. The way my implementation of retroactive looping differs from those in Breadyman or Time Exile’s systems, is that my looper loops MIDI rather than audio. This means that I am only looping musical data, rather than an actual audio recording, giving me a highly malleable starting point to later apply my changes to, since I will just be manipulating midi data, rather than having to run DSP processes on existing audio. Implementing this on the Teensy 4.1 without having much C++ knowledge was a challenge, but I was able to get it working eventually with the help of AI coding tools.. 
Once the Thesis development process began, I revisited my retroactive midi looper and reapproached the UI so that I could make it more flexible and also create the room to be able to add an algorithmic sequencer (like the Torso T-1) alongside the looper. While the initial prototype had one Adafruit 5x6 Neokey Matrix and one quad rotary encoder breakout board, I knew I needed a much more robust UI to support these added features. I bought 2 more Neokey matrices, 3 TCA 8418 key matrix scan ICs to be able to handle this large key matrix I was creating, and 8 more quad rotary encoder breakout boards. To my surprise, integrating these new hardware components ended up being the most time consuming part of the development process. First, I made the mistake of trying to just use 2 TCA8418s to scan my 3 key matrices — I thought that since they each had 18 input pins, I could split the middle matrix between two TCA chips. Because of the nature of how key matrix scanning works, this arrangement caused all sorts of issues like ghosting and phantom key press detection, since neither TCA8418 had a complete picture of the 2nd key matrix. Eventually I realized that each matrix needed its own scan IC, and I resoldered them to their own boards. Still, I was having weird issues with misalignment between the physical key I was pressing and the key press that was being reported. I eventually just had to make a manual key map of each key’s physical location and the key press ID it was being associated with in software, so that I could create a lookup table for the firmware to correctly interpret my key presses. The rotary encoder boards gave me lots of issues as well. I’m not sure if it was just because of the limitations of I2c, or something in my soldering, or something else, but getting all 9 encoder boards to show up and be correctly configured to trigger interrupts in my firmware was a multi-week nightmare. The breakout boards had I2c address jumpers on the back to be able to reconfigure their I2c addresses, and for some reason I had multiple boards start showing up with really odd addresses like 0x02, which is far off from the 0x49 - 0x50 range that these boards are configured for. Sometimes, the addresses would literally change overnight, I would wake up and one of my boards would no longer be responding, and running an I2c scan would show that 0x02 was now 0x03 for some reason! After weeks of troubleshooting these hardware issues, I finally got everything stable enough to begin the task of refactoring my original midi looper firmware to support this new hardware, which also proved to be a non-trivial task. After a few more weeks of work, I was basically back to the place I was at in December of 2024, just with some added UI components and four weeks left to go on my spring Thesis presentations! 

Finally I was able to get moving forward with implementing the new features like the sequencer and the punchline of the project - changes! First, I user tested the project in its current state (just the looper) with some musician/producer friends and I got some valuable feedback. It was challenging for them to trigger a loop at the right time, and they felt that there needed to be a buffer between when you stop playing/inputting notes and when the loop length button is pressed, so there isn’t a stressful rush to press the button. I decided to address this by adding a loop start offset parameter, so that if the loop length button is pressed too late, and an unintended bar of silence is captured for example, the user can scrub back into the loop buffer to window the part of the loop that they intended to capture. This may have been too advanced of a feature for this stage in the project, and a bit of diversion from the important things that were yet to be implemented, but it is useful nonetheless. I also added the ability to change the loop length after the fact (in case the wrong length button was selected), and make it so that subsequent loop layers on the same track could be of different loop lengths than the first layer, which were also both pieces of feedback I received during user testing.

When it came to implementing the sequencer, I luckily had a head start. During our web dev class called Connections Lab, I developed a Tone.js based web groovebox with a Euclidean sequencing engine. This meant that I had an existing playground to test my algorithms in the fast and fluid environment of JavaScript, rather than having to re-compile and re-flash my C++ implementation every time I wanted to make adjustments. I used this web app to develop the unique parameter I had in mind: distribution. Typically with Euclidean sequencing, the patterns can get a little repetitive and can quickly start to feel “Euclidean-y” because the algorithm only affords a certain set of patterns, since it’s all based on even distribution. I wanted a way to break out of these even distribution-only patterns, with a parameter that felt intuitive and meaningful with deterministic behavior. I landed on the idea of distribution — by algorithmically shifting the weight of the pulses towards the beginning or end of the steps, a much wider set of rhythms is accessible by adding only one knob’s worth of control. I tested this idea on the web app and after a few iterations it finally felt right. The next challenge was to port this algorithm from JavaScript to C++, and to decouple the web app’s UI inputs, re-assigning them to physical encoder inputs on the Teensy. This process took roughly a week and a half — much faster than I thought, and it was very exciting once it was successfully implemented — now I could make music faster than ever by just turning a few knobs to build my rhythms, and quickly capturing layers of retroactive loops on top of them.

The final step was to finally implement the main point of this system — the gesture controlled changes. After getting the Wiichuck controller working over I2c, I began to map out the architecture of this macro system. I created s global structure called TrackStates, which contains all of the sequencer and looper parameters for each track. This gave the firmware a central point to be able to manipulate the necessary parameters per-track to make the intended changes. I implemented a global scale system, so that the looper and sequencer content would automatically be in tune with each other, and so that I could apply harmonic changes within this context. I decided on a simple paradigm as a starting point for how these harmonic changes would be accessed via the interface - the key matrix would display a grid of options where the X axis represented the 7 scale degrees and the Y axis represented different chord types within this scale degree. For the energetic changes, I made it even more simple with basically 4 options: increase, decrease, breakdown, and 4 on the floor. After implementing this state-cuing mode, all I needed to do was to get the Wiichuck controller to be the facilitator of these changes. I decided that the Wiichuck controller would be a really useful and expressive way to perform fills and transitions, and mapped it so that when the Z button is pressed and held, the system enters a transition state where the accelerometer affects sequencer pulses and the joystick affect looper octave transposition, so that tilting the joystick and controller quickly changes the patterns in an expressive way. When the Z button is released, the cued up state changed are simultaneously applied, effectively creating a new song section based on a couple of button presses. This system is very fun, fast, and satisfying to play, which is the best case scenario! It’s really interesting to hear how even with this basic implementation, how drastic but musically relevant the shifts can be. 

During my Spring Thesis presentation, I essentially presented 4 possible directions to focus on for the final Thesis development stage in Shanghai: PCB design, industrial design (enclosure and custom components for a compelling looking instrument), continued firmware refinement, and sound design (working on the Ableton Live set that is being controlled so that I can make the best sounding performances with this system). Each piece of feedback from each guest reviewer basically suggested a different direction from this list. My thesis mentor, Chris Kuscinsky from Critter and Guitari, suggested I focus first on developing a PCB, This is the direction that I most agree with currently, because the current prototype of this project is just too unstable to continue working with. The I2c address shifts, the fact that I have to wiggle the protoboards in the header pins just right during bootup to get the TCA8418s and the Wiichuck board correctly communicating over I2c, and the overall janky wire-spaghetti nature of it with many potential points of failure all point to the fact that a custom PCB is a really critical next step. It’s not worth developing an enclosure or continuing to refine the firmware on this version, because I’d just continue to lose time to hardware troubleshooting that could be solved with a more stable development platform. So my plan now is to make the system modular, to break it down into individual submodules that communicate with the main Teensy over USB so that I can develop each piece of the system one at a time without having to redesign and re-fabricate the whole thing again every time I want to make a small change. I am leveraging the open-source schematics and PCB files of the Intech Grid MIDI controllers to develop my own PCB, which will act as a velocity sensitive MIDI keyboard, an encoder bank for sequencer control, and a touch surface for expressive modulation. Once this board is produced and assembled and the firmware is refactored to read this new set of inputs, I will move towards the other paths. I am also taking Parametric design which will be the perfect opportunity to begin developing a custom enclosure for these boards.
